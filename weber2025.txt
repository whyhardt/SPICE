Continuous Predictive Inference Task — Method Summary
Weber, Rascu, Foucault, Li, [...], Klein-Flugge, Hunt (2025 draft)
"From trials to continuous paradigms: efficient, reliable EEG biomarkers of predictive inference in individual participants"

========================================================================
1. GOAL
========================================================================

The task is designed to study predictive inference (how people form predictions,
detect changepoints, and update beliefs under uncertainty) using a continuous
rather than trial-based paradigm. The core innovation is maximising the density
of belief-updating events per unit time: laser beam emissions occur every ~300ms
on average, yielding approximately 600 prediction error events per 3-minute
block. This represents a roughly tenfold increase in event density compared to
conventional trial-based predictive inference tasks (where the outcome phase
occurs every 2-3 seconds). The primary motivation is to obtain reliable
individual-level EEG temporal response functions (TRFs) of belief updating from
brief recordings, enabling practical use as biomarkers in clinical populations.

========================================================================
2. TASK DESIGN
========================================================================

Cover story. Participants are instructed to "Save the World" by protecting a
planet from radioactive laser beams. They move a shield (circular arc) around
a circle on screen using left/right keypresses to catch laser beams emitted
from the centre of the screen.

Generative process. Laser beams are emitted at frequent but variable intervals
with a median inter-beam interval of 300ms. The angular position of each beam
on the circle is drawn from a Gaussian distribution (wrapped modulo 360 deg).
The mean of this distribution remains fixed for several seconds until a
"changepoint" occurs, at which point the mean shifts to a new location. The
variance (stochasticity/noise) and changepoint frequency (volatility) of the
distribution are fixed within each 3-minute block but vary between blocks.

Prediction error. The prediction error on each beam event is the angular
distance between the laser beam landing position and the current centre of the
participant's shield. Unlike trial-based versions where participants explicitly
report their estimate of the mean after each observation, here participants
continuously and freely adjust their shield position at any time.

Factorial manipulation (2x2 design):
  - Changepoint frequency (volatility): high vs. low
  - Noise (stochasticity): high vs. low
  This yields four conditions. Participants complete two 3-minute blocks per
  condition, for a total of eight blocks (24 minutes of recording per session).
  The current condition is indicated by the colour of the central "radioactive
  laser source," an association pre-learned during behavioural training.

Expected behavioural effects:
  - High volatility (more changepoints): faster changepoint detection, steeper
    integration kernels, and higher weight on the most recent sample.
  - High noise (more stochasticity): slower changepoint detection, shallower
    integration kernels, and greater integration over previous samples.
  These two factors have opposing effects on belief updating, and manipulating
  them independently allows dissociation in both behaviour and neural signals.

========================================================================
3. PARTICIPANTS AND STUDY TIMELINE
========================================================================

The data come from a larger pharmacological study contrasting behaviour and EEG
during and after ketamine vs. placebo (saline) infusion. The present manuscript
analyses only baseline data (before infusion) to characterise average effects
and test-retest reliability.

Sample: 38 healthy volunteers total, 8 excluded (3 pilots, 4 side effects from
ketamine infusion, 1 hypertensive episode), leaving a final sample of 30
participants (15 female, ages 18-40, M=24.63, SD=5.43). Paid 250 GBP plus
performance bonus up to 50 GBP. Approved by University of Oxford CUREC3
(Ref: R77437/RE001).

Timeline (3 visits):
  - Visit 1: Screening visit.
  - Visit 2 and 3 (1 week apart, counterbalanced by gender): Infusion visits
    (ketamine or placebo). Each visit includes a baseline recording session
    (before infusion) used for test-retest analyses.
  - 3 days of online training before each infusion visit: task instructions,
    two short training blocks (intermediate volatility/noise) on day 1, four
    blocks (one of each condition) on days 2 and 3. Training teaches the
    association between laser source colour and environmental condition.

========================================================================
4. BEHAVIOURAL MEASURES
========================================================================

Three behavioural indices of belief updating are used to assess sensitivity
to changepoint frequency and stochasticity:

(a) Reaction time to changepoints. Defined as the time taken for the shield
    position to cross halfway (in angular space) from the pre-changepoint mean
    to the post-changepoint mean. This measure is robust against small
    adjustments near the old mean and failure to fully reach the new mean.
    Results: faster under high volatility, faster under low noise.

(b) Integration kernels. A regression model estimates the lagged influence of
    the five most recent signed prediction errors on the decision to move the
    shield. The difference in weight between the (t-1) and (t-2) beam captures
    the "steepness" of recency weighting.
    Results: steeper under high volatility, steeper under low noise.

(c) Peri-changepoint learning rates. The learning rate for each beam event is
    the fractional movement of the shield toward the beam location. Peri-
    changepoint learning rates are computed by locking to each changepoint and
    measuring the transient increase.
    Results: evoked learning rate change is larger under low volatility and
    under low noise.

All three behavioural measures show medium-to-strong test-retest reliability
across sessions one week apart.

========================================================================
5. EEG ANALYSIS APPROACH
========================================================================

Because of the high event density (~300ms inter-beam interval), neural
responses to successive beam events overlap in time. A deconvolutional general
linear modelling (GLM) approach is used to unmix these overlapping EEG
responses, estimating temporal response functions (TRFs) to prediction error
events from -200ms to 1000ms around each laser emission.

Parametric modulators in the GLM:
  (i)   Sensory change: absolute angular distance from beam(t-1) to beam(t).
  (ii)  Belief prediction error: absolute angular distance from beam(t) to
        the current shield position.
  (iii) Signed belief prediction error: signed (left/right) angular distance
        from beam(t) to the shield position.

Key EEG findings:
  - Beam onset evokes a visual negativity ~110ms post-onset at occipital
    electrode Oz.
  - A later positive-going potential (250-330ms, central electrode Cz) is
    sensitive to sensory change magnitude, consistent with P300/sensory
    surprise.
  - Belief prediction error yields a triphasic potential most prominent over
    centroparietal electrodes (CPz), modulated by both volatility and
    stochasticity.
  - Signed belief prediction error is lateralised (e.g., right-hemisphere
    sensors for left-handed button press).

========================================================================
6. TEST-RETEST RELIABILITY
========================================================================

TRFs were measured in two baseline sessions one week apart. Despite inter-
individual variation in waveform morphology, TRFs were highly consistent
within participants across sessions.

Intraclass correlation (ICC) results:
  - Using 24 minutes of EEG: ICCs approach 0.9 for all four TRF components
    (beam onset, sensory change, belief prediction error, signed belief PE).
  - Using 6 minutes of EEG: ICC > 0.75 for all components (high reliability).
  - Using 3 minutes of EEG: ICC ~ 0.75 (adequate reliability).

This demonstrates that the high event density of the continuous paradigm
enables reliable neurophysiological markers of predictive inference at the
individual level from brief recordings.

========================================================================
7. USABILITY AND CLINICAL TRANSLATION
========================================================================

Key usability features:
  - Minimal training required (3 days of short online sessions before lab
    visit), unlike previous continuous paradigms that required extensive
    training (several hours).
  - Brief recording duration: reliable measures from as few as 6 minutes of
    EEG data.
  - Simple instructions and intuitive cover story ("Save the World").
  - Can be paired with a conventional auditory mismatch negativity (MMN)
    paradigm running in the background, providing a second pre-attentive
    marker of predictive inference with known high test-retest reliability.
  - Documented online repository with task code, analysis scripts, and
    deployment guidance.

Clinical relevance:
  - Predictive inference alterations have been implicated in schizophrenia and
    psychosis (aberrant precision weighting, "jump to conclusions," reduced
    MMN), autism spectrum conditions (atypical weighting of priors vs. sensory
    evidence), OCD and compulsivity (excessive information gathering, altered
    learning rates), and neurodegenerative conditions such as Lewy body
    dementia (visual hallucinations from failures of perceptual inference).
  - The 2x2 factorial design enables dissociation of volatility and noise
    effects, which may differ across clinical populations.
  - High test-retest reliability supports within-subject longitudinal designs
    (tracking changes over time or in response to intervention), which the
    authors argue is more appropriate for clinical translation than cross-
    sectional between-group comparisons.

========================================================================
8. KEY DESIGN PARAMETERS (where specified)
========================================================================

  - Inter-beam interval: median 300ms (variable)
  - Block duration: 3 minutes
  - Blocks per condition: 2
  - Total conditions: 4 (2x2: volatility x noise)
  - Total recording time per session: 24 minutes (8 blocks)
  - Beam distribution: Gaussian, wrapped modulo 360 deg
  - Changepoint structure: mean shifts to new location after several seconds
  - Shield control: left/right keypress (clockwise/counter-clockwise)
  - TRF window: -200ms to 1000ms relative to beam onset
  - Shield width, movement speed, exact changepoint rates, and distribution
    variances per condition: not specified in this draft (noted as TBD in the
    methods section)
